 Current lifestyle and habits govern the risk of cardiovascular diseases later in life. Arteries can begin accumulating plaque as early as childhood and adolescence, which is why even young and middle-aged people are developing heart ailments.
 
 
 
 Individuals who develop high blood pressure may not experience symptoms, which is why it is known as the ‘silent killer’. Rather than waiting for the body to alert you with other dangerous signs, test your blood pressure regularly. This disorder should not be left untreated as it can develop into more serious conditions like stroke,  , and kidney issues.
 
 
 
 Heart rate can vary over the course of a day depending on an individual’s activity level, like exercise. 
 However, an irregular heartbeat can signal arrhythmia.
 
 
 
 If an individual feels that she/he is experiencing a heart attack, the doctor or hospital emergency must be called at once. Emergency services can treat or even resuscitate if the heart stops midway. Resting and waiting will only aggravate the situation.
 
 
 
 Though chest pain is the most common sign of a heart attack, it’s one of the symptoms. Shortness of breath, nausea, sweating, light-headedness or pain in various parts of upper body could also signal a heart attack.
 
 
 Drinking is not encouraged among non-drinkers to counter the risks of heart disease, and those who drink must limit alcohol intake to no more than two drinks a day (men) and one drink for women.
