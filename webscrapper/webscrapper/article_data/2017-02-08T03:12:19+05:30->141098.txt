 “The offence is very serious and against the national economy. In the case of counterfeiting, the custody of accused is necessary. On the basis of custodial interrogation only, it is possible for the police to investigate further,” said Justice Bhatkar and pointed out that there was evidence against the accused justify his custody.
 
 On December 22, 2016, based on a tip off, the police laid a trap on the Mumbai-Agra highway near Adgoan. A search of three cars led to the seizure of fake currency notes with a total face value of Rs 1.35 crore. The notes were of Rs 500 and Rs 1,000 denomination. Sawkar's lawyers claimed that he was falsely implicated in the case, as he had not been arrested when the notes were seized and his name did not feature in the FIR. Sawkar claimed he was not involved in the racket of making fake notes. 
 
 Additional public prosecutor Sooraj Hulke opposed the anticipatory bail application. The prosecutor claimed the police had information that Sawkar had allegedly purchased the printer, paper cutting machine and ink bottle and was involved in making the fake Rs 1,000 and Rs 500 currency notes. “The police needed to interrogate Sawkar in custody to find out where and how the fake currency notes were printed,” said advocate Hulke.
